01 · GENERAL

The basics, in plain language.

What EcoHubs is, who's behind it, and how to read the rest of this site.

What is EcoHubs, in one paragraph? +
EcoHubs is an online community writing the Blueprint for a different way to live and work together — and, eventually, a network of small physical hubs that put it into practice. We're online-first today; physical pilots come next, when communities are ready.
Who is behind this? +
A founder-led core right now, transitioning toward community governance. Members shape the Blueprint, vote on proposals, and decide together who joins next. Names, roles, and decision logs are public inside ecohubsOS.
Is this a non-profit, a company, or a DAO? +
A hybrid by design — a stewarding entity that holds legal responsibility, with community-decided governance for direction, content, and membership. The legal and governance details are written into the Blueprint and reviewed in the open.
Where are you based? +
Everywhere members are. The community is online and globally distributed. The first physical pilot hub is in Ecuador. More will follow as the Blueprint matures and member-led local groups emerge.
How can I follow along without joining? +
Read the Blueprint — it's open. Subscribe to the field-notes letter for monthly updates from inside the community. No tracking, no engagement loop, easy to unsubscribe.
Is the project funded? By whom? +
Funding today comes from founder capital and aligned grants. We publish the funding sources and constraints openly. We do not take investment that requires speculative returns on community participation.
02 · VISION

What we're trying to do, and what we're not.

The why under the work — and the limits of what we claim.

What problem are you actually trying to solve? +
Modern life has fragmented belonging, work, and place. We're building a pattern where small communities can hold all three together — locally, regeneratively, and without rebuilding from scratch each time.
Is this a utopia, an eco-village, or a co-living brand? +
None of those. We borrow from all of them and refuse the parts that didn't work. Not a utopia — utopias are brittle. Not a co-living brand — we're not selling lifestyle. The closest honest description is a network of small communities sharing an open standard.
What does success look like in 10 years? +
Many small hubs around the world running the Blueprint in their own way, with a living, well-maintained shared standard between them. Success is not size — it's resilience and honest replication.
What are your stances on politics, religion, ideology? +
We have stances — on ecology, dignity, transparency, non-extraction — and they're written into the Blueprint. We don't have a doctrine you must agree with. We expect disagreement; we ask for honest disagreement.
How is this different from intentional communities of the past? +
We start from an open, versioned standard — not a charismatic founder. Decisions are logged, governance is explicit, and conflict-repair is a chapter, not a private conversation. Past projects often failed where these were missing.
What could go wrong, honestly? +
The Blueprint could ossify; the founder phase could overstay its welcome; pilots could underdeliver. We name these risks in the open and write them into the working notes — being honest about failure modes is part of how we keep them survivable.
03 · BLUEPRINT

The shared document, how it actually works.

What's in it, who edits it, and what licence it carries.

What exactly is the Blueprint? +
A living, versioned document of patterns: how a hub starts, how it governs, how it shares value, how it repairs conflict, how it leaves room for difference. It's a standard, not a script — read it like infrastructure, not a manifesto.
Who can edit it? +
Members. Anyone can propose changes; substantive edits go through review and a community vote before merging. Drafts, comments, and the full edit history are visible inside ecohubsOS.
Is it open source? What licence? +
Yes — published under a permissive open licence (CC BY-SA for the writing, MIT for the tooling). Other communities are welcome to adopt, fork, and adapt it. Attribution and share-alike are how the standard stays a standard.
How are chapters structured? +
Each chapter follows the same shape: the question it answers, the pattern we propose, the trade-offs, the worked examples, the open issues. If a chapter doesn't have all five, it's still a draft.
How do you handle disagreement on a chapter? +
Disagreement is treated as material, not noise. Strong objections become "open issues" attached to the chapter and stay visible until resolved. Sometimes that means two patterns coexist — one isn't always right.
What's the current version, and how often does it change? +
The Blueprint is on a slow release cadence — minor revisions weekly, named versions roughly twice a year. Each version is tagged, archived, and citable. Hubs can pin to a version and upgrade deliberately.
Can my community adopt only parts of it? +
Yes. The Blueprint is modular — you can adopt the governance chapter without the value-accounting chapter, or vice versa. We'd love to hear which patterns you take and which you leave; that feedback shapes the next version.
04 · MEMBERSHIP

Joining, contributing, and stepping back.

The application, the day-to-day, and the dignified way out.

Is there a fee to join? +
No. Membership is free and contribution-based. Trust, access, and influence grow with what you bring — not what you pay.
How long does the application take? +
About 20 minutes for you, then a 3-day community review. The community itself reads applications and decides together — there's no auto-approval and no founder veto.
Do I need to be technical or understand Web3? +
No. Members include permaculturists, parents, facilitators, designers, educators, listeners. Tools support coordination — they don't gatekeep it.
What does a typical week look like as a member? +
A few async threads in the rooms you care about, an optional weekly call, occasional proposal reviews and votes. Most members put in two to five hours a week. Some put in much more during a chapter they care about; some less in quieter seasons. Both are fine.
What is ECO, and is it a cryptocurrency? +
ECO is an internal value unit — non-transferable, non-tradeable, never speculative. It marks contribution and unlocks access and responsibility inside the community. Not an investment, not a coin to flip.
Do I have to move somewhere to be a member? +
No. The community is online-first. Physical hubs come later, when communities are ready, and they're optional. You can be a deeply involved member without ever stepping onto a hub.
Can I leave? What happens to my contributions? +
Yes, any time. Step back, exit, return — the Blueprint includes clear, dignified paths for all of those. Your contributions remain credited; your personal data leaves with you on request.
What happens if my application isn't accepted? +
You'll hear back with the actual reason — not a form letter. Sometimes it's timing, sometimes it's a fit question, sometimes it's "come back after you've read X chapter." We answer everyone, either way.
How does conflict between members get handled? +
Through the conflict-repair chapter of the Blueprint — a written protocol with named steps: direct conversation, facilitated circle, community-supported repair, and, where needed, structured separation. None of it is private; the protocol is.
